Becoming a Game Creator "I Want to Make Games"IwataI'm here today to talk with Hazama-san, a producer at Square Enix, about his latest project, THEATRHYTHM FINAL FANTASY. Thank you for coming.HazamaThank you. IwataThanks. I'd like to begin by talking about your experience in video games so far. We'll get on to the game itself eventually, but I often find that the experiences and thought processes of the creator of a product are reflected in the product itself.HazamaOkay.IwataWhat was your first experience of video games?HazamaIf you mean playing them, I remember that my friends and I used to go to the arcade a lot on the way home from high school. Before that, I played console games from junior high school age onwards, but I didn't have a console of my own, so that was always at friends' houses. (Editor's note: in Japan, pupils attend high school between the ages of 15 and 18, and junior high school between 12 and 15.) IwataWhat kind of games did you play?HazamaI was obsessed with Mario Bros.1. I would just keep playing and playing until the mother of whichever friend it was would come and say, "Don't you think your parents will be worried about you?" Then I would just grab the cartridge and move on to the next friend's house... 1. Mario Bros. was an action game released on NES and in the arcades by Nintendo in Japan in 1983. IwataIt was always a real scramble for the controller in those days, wasn't it?HazamaYes! (laughs) I think it was the same in houses everywhere. Yes, I think that's one of my earliest memories of video games.IwataIs there anything in particular that has stuck with you from those early experiences?HazamaI suppose that as a child I always saw games as something that had to be enjoyed with friends. I never thought of them any other way. It was probably because, like I said before, we never had a console at home.IwataWere you not allowed one?HazamaI think that was it, yes... It's the first time I've ever had to talk about myself like this! (laughs) Nowadays, I get along with my parents very well, but back then, they were pretty strict.IwataI see.HazamaI suppose that was why, when I first moved out of home, I spent all the money I earned on doing the things I had never been allowed to do before.IwataWhat kind of things?HazamaIt seems so silly now... It was just little things like having bubble baths like the ones I'd seen in the movies, that sort of thing. (laughs) I mean, the best I could expect at our house from my parents was that the bath salts allegedly included the same ingredients as those of famous hot springs, you know? (Editor's note: In Japan, it is much more common to use bath salts than bubble bath -bubble baths are generally considered to be more of an indulgent treat.) IwataI know exactly how you felt! (laughs)HazamaWhat else...? Oh, watching TV in the bath, that sort of thing. I sound rather obsessed with baths! (laughs) Anyway, yeah, it was mostly that sort of thing.IwataI suppose you were just enjoying the feeling of having nobody to tell you what to do.HazamaExactly. So it wasn't long before I ended up buying my first console. I bought myself a SNES, and a copy of Final Fantasy VI2. 2. Final Fantasy VI was the sixth installment in the Final Fantasy series, and was released by Squaresoft (now Square Enix) for the SNES in Japan in 1994. IwataSo that was your first encounter with Final Fantasy? HazamaYes. I took it home, plugged it into the TV, popped in the cartridge, and grabbed up the controller thinking, "Let's do this!" Then I saw that opening sequence... That was a really pivotal moment for me.IwataIt was so much more impressive than what had come before, wasn't it?HazamaYes. It was amazing. The credits appearing while those Magitek Armor suits walked through the snow like something out of a movie... It was the first time I'd really sat down with a game like that, and it was the first one I'd ever bought. I was instantly hooked.IwataSo that was the moment that video games captured your heart, right?HazamaYes. I was working for Bandai (now Namco Bandai) in the games section at the time, but I was in procurement, so I never imagined I would one day be working for the company that made the game I was playing.IwataThat's not a department that has any relationship with development teams, is it?HazamaNo. It was all about when certain products had to be in certain warehouses, things like that - dealing with the finished product, basically. But I think it was around that time that I first started to think, maybe only faintly at first, that I might want to get involved with making games. But it didn't happen overnight...IwataIt's difficult without an "in" of some kind though, isn't it? Some people are especially good at drawing, or good at programming, and get their start from there, don't they?HazamaFrom that perspective I think - although my title today is a producer and I love games - I am not a creator. But I did start feeling very strongly around that time that I wanted to be involved somehow, even if I wasn't creating games myself.IwataI see. And I suppose, even if you're not involved in production, even if you're in procurement or whatever, you still have to know your product extremely well, so that you know how many copies to order and things like that.HazamaAbsolutely.IwataYou have to know how certain products have been received, otherwise you might make a mistake, and leave the company with a mountain of surplus. And I suppose while doing that, you naturally start thinking, "If only this game had been designed differently, like this," or, "People really respond to this." That kind of thing?HazamaYes. There was plenty of that.IwataWithout that, I suppose you wouldn't be able to look at your products the right way.HazamaNo.IwataAnd without such a list in your mind, even when you do end up on the development side, you cannot propose these ideas easily.HazamaWell, no. If I look back, I'd say absolutely not.IwataBut it's still interesting - when I have conversations like this, it makes me realize that there's no such thing as wasted experience. Everything has its use further down the line.HazamaYes, absolutely.IwataI often think that, even if something isn't particularly enjoyable at the time, it will eventually serve me well.HazamaOh yes, completely! It's amazing... Everything you're saying, I'm thinking, "Yes, that's me he's talking about!"(both laugh)IwataSo was that the reason you ended up at Squaresoft (now Square Enix)?HazamaIt was certainly part of it. I'd been in charge of procurement at Bandai for five years, and (Shinji) Hashimoto3, who is now a corporate officer of the company, invited me to join. Actually, you could say, he tricked me into joining... (laughs) 3. Shinji Hashimoto is the Corporate Executive of the 1st Production Department. He began as a producer at the original Squaresoft, where he oversaw many titles, including Final Fantasy VII. IwataHe did? How? (laughs)HazamaWell, because I was joining Squaresoft, I obviously thought I would be working with games, but as soon as I joined, they started a merchandising section, which I was put in charge of. They must have thought because I'd worked for Bandai, I'd be good with character goods and toys and all that stuff. (laughs)Iwata(laughs) "You! Make us some great merchandise!" That kind of thing? But you were in game procurement at Bandai, weren't you? You didn't have any experience of making merchandising products.HazamaNo. But that meant it was all new experience for me. I didn't mind at all, and I got to help out with lots of other things while I was doing it.IwataI see.HazamaAnd then, one day, (Tetsuya) Nomura4 and I were having a chat, and he mentioned that almost everyone who joined the company, no matter what department they ended up in, loved games and had wanted to make them at some point, and I remembered, "Oh, yeah. That used to be me, tooâ¦" 4. Tetsuya Nomura created many of the most beloved Final Fantasy characters as a character designer, while as a director, he is responsible for the Kingdom Hearts series of games. IwataAnd after hearing Nomura-san's comment, suddenly what had been a vague idea became a concrete desire?HazamaExactly. Suddenly it all clicked into place, and I realized I was in just the right place to realize my dream.IwataSo a casual word from Nomura-san changed the path your life would eventually take...
